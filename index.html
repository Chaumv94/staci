The 7 Steps to a Perfectly Level Yard

1

Plan & Prepare

First, clear the area of all debris, rocks, and existing vegetation. Check for any utility lines before you dig! Mark out the project area with stakes and string.

Helpful Tip: Call 811 before you dig to have utility lines marked for free. Safety first!

2

Map Your Slope

Hammer wooden stakes into the ground in a grid pattern across your work area (about 10 feet apart). These will be your reference points for leveling.

Watch Tutorial Read More
3

Find Your Level

Tie a string to a stake in a high area. Use a line level to get the string perfectly level, then attach it to the next stake. Mark the level line on each stake. Repeat for the entire grid.

You're doing great! This step is key to getting a professional-looking result.
Watch Tutorial Read More
4

Remove High Spots

Using your marked stakes as a guide, use a shovel or tiller to remove soil from the high spots. Move this soil to the low spots or remove it from the yard.

Watch Tutorial Read More
5

Fill Low Spots

Add topsoil to the low-lying areas. A good mix is 60% sand, 40% topsoil. Add soil in layers, lightly tamping it down as you go to prevent major settling.

Staci's Tip: If you have clay-heavy soil, mixing in some compost along with the sand will really improve drainage.
Watch Tutorial Read More
6

Rake & Grade

Use a large landscape rake to smooth the entire surface. Use the back of the rake to create a final, even grade. Eyeball it from different angles to check for consistency.

7

Water & Settle

Lightly water the entire area. This helps the soil settle. You may notice new low spots appear. Fill them in, re-rake, and let the area dry before planting grass or landscaping.

Almost there! This final step ensures your hard work pays off with a perfectly settled lawn.

Frequently Asked Questions

Here are a few common questions that might pop up. You're not alone in wondering!

How do I ensure proper drainage? +

For proper drainage, you should create a very slight slope (about 1-2%) away from your house foundation. This means for every 100 feet of distance, the ground should drop by 1-2 feet. You can achieve this by adjusting your string lines accordingly.

Do I need to rent any heavy equipment? +

For very large or severely sloped yards, renting a small skid steer or a heavy-duty tiller can save you a tremendous amount of time and physical effort. For smaller, more manageable areas, a shovel and wheelbarrow will suffice.

What's the best time of year to do this? +

Spring and early fall are ideal. The weather is typically mild, and the soil is not too hard or too wet. This also gives new grass seed the best chance to germinate and establish before extreme heat or cold sets in.

Troubleshooting

Don't worry if you hit a snag. Every project has them! Here's how to solve common issues.

My soil is very compacted and hard to dig. +

Water the area thoroughly a day or two before you plan to dig. This will soften the ground significantly. If it's still too difficult, renting a garden tiller is your best option to break up the compacted soil.

After watering, I have puddles forming. +

Puddles indicate low spots you may have missed or areas with poor drainage (heavy clay soil). Wait for the area to dry, then add more topsoil/sand mixture to these spots and rake them smooth. Amending the soil with compost can also improve drainage over time.
